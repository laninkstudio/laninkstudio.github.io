Chinese Calligraphy Styles Explained: Which One Is Right for Your Tattoo?

Seal Script (篆书)
  • Origin: The oldest formal script, evolving from bronze inscriptions (c. 1600–256 BCE).
  • Characteristics: Symmetrical, rounded lines with uniform thickness; resembles ancient engraved seals.
  • Best for: Stamps, minimal designs, and those who want a mysterious look (as it is hard for modern readers to decipher).

Clerical Script (隶书)
  • Origin: Developed during the Qin–Han dynasties (c. 221 BCE–220 CE) to simplify Seal Script.
  • Characteristics: Flattened structure with broad, horizontal strokes ending in "wave-like" tails.
  • Best for: Elegant, minimal, historical, or vintage aesthetics.

Regular Script (楷书)
  • Origin: Matured in the Wei–Jin periods (c. 220–420 CE).
  • Characteristics: Precise, square strokes with clear structure; the most legible script.
  • Best for: When clarity and structure are your top priority.

Semi-Cursive (行书)
  • Origin: Emerged as a cursive form of Regular Script during the Han–Jin dynasties (c. 200–400 CE).
  • Characteristics: Flowing, smooth, artistic. Strokes flow continuously but remain readable.
  • Best for: Showing movement, freedom, and artistic flair. A popular choice for tattoo lovers who want movement and energy.

Cursive Script (草书)
  • Origin: Developed from Clerical Script around the Han dynasty (c. 200 CE).
  • Characteristics: Highly abstract and fluid; strokes are simplified or merged for speed and artistic effect.
  • Best for: Wild, expressive, emotional — but only recommended if you want something abstract and you understand the meaning beforehand. (as it is difficult to read without training).
